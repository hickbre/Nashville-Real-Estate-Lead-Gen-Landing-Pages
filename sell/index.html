Before you list

Three things most sellers learn too late

01

The Overpricing Trap

Buyers search in price brackets online, and smart pricing lives on the bridge between two of them. Price at $1.265M and you only capture buyers searching from $1.25M and up. Price at $1.25M and you capture both the buyers searching up to $1.25M AND the buyers starting their search at $1.25M, doubling your potential audience before a single showing. More eyes means more competition. More competition means more leverage. Pricing above a bracket means less eyes on your home.

02

The Stale Listing Effect

After approximately 21 days on the market, buyers start to assume something is wrong with your home. That's just the psychology of this market, coming out of a period where the majority of homes moved in days. There may be absolutely nothing wrong with the property, the sale cycle is simply slower right now, but perception becomes reality in negotiation. A stale listing invites low offers, demands for concessions, and the kind of leverage shift that costs you money. Price reductions only make it worse, signaling desperation rather than value.

03

The Bidding War Math

A $1.15M list price that attracts multiple offers and closes at $1.21M beats a $1.25M list that grinds down to $1.18M after 77 days on market, costs you two months of carrying costs, and a lot of stress along the way. That's not a theory, we've seen it happen many times, and this simulator will help show you in real time.

Pricing scenario assumptions: Scenario A (Smart Seller Advantage) models a list price at 95% of your estimated value with a final sale at 101% of estimated value in 10 days. Scenario B (Standard) models list at 100%, close at 99%, 35 days on market. Scenario C (Gamble) models list at 104%, close at 97%, 75 days on market. These are modeling assumptions for illustrative purposes — actual market outcomes vary based on property condition, neighborhood demand, seasonality, and marketing execution.

Closing costs: Estimated at 0.5% of sale price, which covers typical title, attorney, and closing fees assuming an average 50/50 split of title charges between buyer and seller. The largest seller title cost is typically Owner's Title Insurance, which is customarily paid by the seller in Tennessee but is fully negotiable in the offer. Transfer taxes (state and county) are customarily paid by the buyer in Tennessee unless negotiated otherwise, and are not deducted from the seller's net in this tool. Your actual closing cost responsibilities will be detailed in your listing agreement and settlement statement.

Commission: Commissions are fully negotiable and vary. The default shown (3% listing + 3% buyer's agent = 6%) reflects a typical Nashville-area structure but is not a quoted rate for any specific transaction.
